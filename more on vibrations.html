More on Vibrations

Just as how notes separated by an octave blend together well due to the simple ratio of their frequencies, notes whose ratios of frequencies can simplify to have low numbers in the numerator and denominator tend to combine to make a pleasant sound. When notes harmonize in this way, they are said to be consonant.

Although consonance often sounds soothing, composers often hope to create tension in their music. It is in these situations that dissonance, or a lack of harmony between notes, is preferred to consonance for its ability to create contrast and emotion. Dissonance is created when notes’ ratios of frequencies cannot be simplified to have low numbers in the numerator and denominator.

The reason that different instruments make different sounds even when playing the same notes is because when instruments play a note, they not only play that frequency but also play overtones which introduce other frequencies to their sounds. For example, a piano’s sound is dominated by its fundamental tone, whereas a flute’s first harmonic overtone is louder than its fundamental tone.
